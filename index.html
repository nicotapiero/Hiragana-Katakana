Tricky ones

The issue with trying to use purely mnemonics is that your "image" might line up with some other characters. Make sure to learn the similar looking ones to avoid this happening as much as possible:

Similar looking Hiragana:

  • Face looking bois:

    Verさち ((ver) sa/chi)
    • Kinda close: き (ki) and も (mo) and even ま (ma) to an extent
    • Also ら (ra) looks like their cousin
  • Fishing ones:
    れ re / わ wa / ね ne
    (ready to fish / waaaa / net a fish)
  • Sideways U looking ones:
    う(u) vs つ (tsu)
  • "Knee" looking ones:
    The mnemonic for に (ni) is supposed to be "knee", but if you are thinking about "knee" any of these could also look like knees:
    • い (i)
    • り (ri) け (ke)
    • は (ha) ほ (ho)
  • T bois:
    た (ta) な (na)
  • 3 looking boys:
    る (ru) ろ (ro)

Similar looking Katakana:

  • Smiley Boys:
    A clever way to remember these two is to picture their hiragana counterparts つ (tsu) and し (shi). ツ tsu / シ shi
  • One eye-d smiley boys:
    ソ so / ン n

Font practice:

It's really important to practice looking at the characters in different fonts, especially if you're trying to learn the mnemonics. Serifs vs sans-serif can throw you off if you're not ready for it (just look at お!), so make sure to study both! Press the buttons to change the fonts, and then study the wikipedia charts I stole below (or even go back up and read the previous section with the new font):

When you're ready, also make sure to study going from sound → character. This isn't necessary, but can help strengthen the mental connections for characters/sounds when reading as well (mnemonics help for looking at a character and recognizing it, but what aboout being able to draw a "knee" or "netting a fish"?). This will be necessary for writing though, but at that point you'll also need to learn stroke order.
